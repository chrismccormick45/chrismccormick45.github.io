Experience

Taira Lab: Undergraduate Research Assistant

During the 2023 spring quarter, one of my professors reached out to me to ask if I would work for his Computational and Data-Driven Fluid Dynamics lab ( Taira Lab ) over the summer. In this job, I got to convert CFD simulation data into a 3D-printable file. This taught me a lot about how to analyze and manipulate CFD data. I wrote code to import flow data, interpolate it onto a fine 3D point grid, remove discontinuities in the flow, project a color contour onto the flow, and export it as a fully colored 3D-printable file. After running the code with different sets of data, I printed the flows with my own personal 3D printer, allowing me to further explore 3D printing and learn how to mitigate errors to get the best print. RC Drone Design & Assembly

This summer, I designed and assembled a 5-inch RC drone from scratch. My goal with this project was to have fun exploring rotary-wing aircraft and to refine my design skills!

I wanted to customize the design of my drone frame, so I decided to 3D model it in SolidWorks and 3D print it. While working on the frame design, I conducted lots of research on each individual component of the drone. I learned a lot about why certain component attributes, like motor KV or battery cell count, are desired under a set of circumstances. I took lots of notes and used all my research to rigorously select my drone components. After finishing the assembly, configuring the drone on my computer, and test flying the drone a few times, I redesigned specific parts of the frame that needed work and tested different propellers for smoother flight.

I am currently working on getting my HAM Amateur Radio License so that I can mount a camera and VTX chip on the drone. This will allow me to watch live video feed from my drone!

Engineering Course: E96A Plane Design

During the 2023 Winter Quarter at UCLA, I enrolled in a voluntary, hands-on airplane engineering course. In this class, students were broken into teams to design, test, and assemble a remote controlled airplane. I assumed the lead role on my team, and helped divide up tasks among the team members.

After settling on a general plane design, my team ran instructor-provided MATLAB code to determine the general weight, size, and wingspan of our airplane. We rigorously selected the airfoils for our wing and stabilizers using these attributes, the coefficients of lift and drag of different airfoils, and the goals we wanted to achieve. After creating and assembling our entire airplane in SolidWorks, we continuously added, subtracted, and redesigned different components throughout the quarter. This was because, after assembling and testing different parts of our airplane, we found certain designs superior to others. When we were finished, it was incredibly rewarding to see our hard work pay off when our airplane flew and set new records.

In addition to making history by successfully designing the first twin-prop airplane ever to built in the E96A Planes course, my team won the class award for designing the fastest plane.

Engineering Course: E96R Rocket Engineering

During the 2022 Fall Quarter at UCLA, I enrolled in a voluntary, hands-on rocket engineering course. In this course, students formed teams and were tasked with designing, testing, and assembling two solid motor rockets. The first rocket used a C-class rocket motor and the second rocket used a stronger G-class rocket motor.

In designing these rockets, each team member conducted their own independent research and introduced ideas to implement on our designs. Throughout each design process, we experimented with different materials and redesigned our rockets accordingly. For example, our first rocket was fully 3D printed, while our second rocket utilized fiberglass and wood to help reduce weight. In the end, after launching our first rocket, learning from minor design mistakes, and adding new features to our second rocket, our second rocket exceeded our expectations on launch by achieving an apogee of about 3200 ft. This rocket is the rocket displayed in the images to the right (please forgive the poor-quality flight data at the end).

Design, Build, Fly at UCLA

During the 2022-23 school year, I joined the manufacturing team of Design/Build/Fly (DBF) at UCLA. In this club, students are challenged to design a plane capable of completing a series of missions laid out by the intercollegiate AIAA Design/Build/Fly Competition. Though I initially joined the aerodynamics team and used SolidWorks, SimScale, and MATLAB to run a few CFD simulations and analyze the corresponding data, I ultimately found myself on the manufacturing team which was much more hands-on and newcomer friendly. While on this team, I helped contribute to the general design of the airplane and helped assemble the stabilizers. After competing in the annual AIAA Design/Build/Fly Competition, our team placed 15th out of 81 competing teams (the highest placement ever achieved by UCLA)!
