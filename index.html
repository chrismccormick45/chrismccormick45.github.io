Experience

Design, Build, Fly at UCLA

At UCLA, I have joined the Design/Build/Fly club. In this club, students are challenged to design a plane capable of completing a series of missions laid out by the intercollegiate AIAA Design/Build/Fly Competition. I decided to join the aerodynamics subteam, and have been working with a group of students to design the nose and the wings of the plane. We have learned how to design different airfoils and aerodynamic surfaces in SolidWorks, how to run CFD simulations on those surfaces, and how to use MATLAB to automate data calculations. Though I am still fairly new, and still need lots of help, I am excited to learn more about plane design and eager to get more involved with this project!

I am also currently in the process of joining the Rocket Project at UCLA, becoming a member of the Prometheus team.

Engineering Course: E96R Rocket Engineering

Also at UCLA, I decided to enroll in a rocket engineering course. In this course, teams of two to five students were tasked with designing, testing, and assembling two model rockets. Using a model rocket design program called OpenRocket, my team settled on a mainstream design for our first rocket. After 3D modeling it in SolidWorks, with a detachable nose cone and deployable parachute, we then completely 3D printed the rocket. However, when launch came, the results were lack luster and we did not achieve anywhere near the apogee and speed that we expected.

When designing our second rocket, my team wanted to do better. So, each team member conducted their own independent research and suggested ideas to the group. We incorporated parts of the first rocket that worked well, like the number and shape of the fins, but also added new components and experimented with different materials. We made the body tube out of our own fiberglass lay-up that we created, the fins out of laser cut ply wood, we designed a smaller, more aerodynamic nose cone, we incorporated a new, 3D printed boat tail to increase the speed of our rocket, and also included a new altimeter. I worked on soldering the electrical components of the rocket, helped with deciding the shapes of and 3D modeling of the fins, engine mounts, and nose cone. It was satisfying to see our hard work payoff when our rocket exceeding our expectations, achieving an apogee of about 3200 ft and a max speed of about 0.5 Mach. I am currently enrolled in the engineering course E96A Engineering Design: Planes, where I will conduct similar work but with remote-controlled planes.

Saddleback Robotics

At my previous institution, I joined the robotics club's Mars Rover Team. This team was tasked with the ground-up design and assembly of a radio-controller rover that was capable of navigating rough terrain, driving autonomously, picking up and dragging heavy objects with its mechanical arm, and analyzing dirt samples for signs of life. Though I also helped different subteams with assembly, I decided to join the chassis and electrical subteams. On these subteams, I was assigned to design, 3D model, and assemble different components within the rover's chassis. These components mainly consisted of electrical circuits and their corresponding housing boxes, however I also worked on assembling different components that connected to the rover's drivetrain.
