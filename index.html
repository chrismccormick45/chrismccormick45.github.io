Experience

Design, Build, Fly at UCLA

This school year, 2022-2023, I decided to join the aerodynmaics and the CAD subteams of Design, Build, Fly at UCLA (DBF). DBF is a club at UCLA, where each year we design and construct a remote controlled airplane from the bottom up. Because each year our annual, intercollegiate competition has a different set of requirements, the whole team has frequent general meetings in which each subteam discusses each weeks progress and what they might need done from another subteam. Additionally, we brainstorms about how to best and most efficiently design our airplane. For example, this year our plane is required to have removeable wings that can be reassembled in no more than around 10 mins. Therefore, in our general meetings, we discuss different design ideas as well as how testing of previous designs went. Though this is my first quarter on the team, I feel as though I have already learned so much about plane design and collaboration. As previously mentioned, I was a member of a collaborative engineering project at my community college. However, because this project has many more members and because I have been able to get much more hands-on experience with intricate designs and ideas, DBF has already taught me so much more about the collaboration and the engineering process.

In the aerodynamics subteam, we design the aerodynamic surfaces of the airplane. Though I am still in the "New Member Training" phase, I have already learned how to design a airfoil, how to import it into, and further design it in, SolidWorks from curve points calculated in MS Excel, how to run CFD simulations on airfoils with applications like XFOIL and SimScale, and how to use MATLAB to analyze flight data (from altimeters and speedometers). I look forward to really getting involved with this subteam, and learning more about aerodynamics!

In the CAD subteam, we have are tasked with integrating any design ideas, and getting them ready for either 3D printing or laser cutting (if applicable). Additionally, when the whole team settles on a design, we create each part in SolidWorks and assemble the plane prior to the plane's actual construction. This is to ensure that all the plane components fit together as designed, and to allow the team to have a visual of how each component connection should fit together and how they should appear during and after construction.

Engineering Course: E96R ------ STILL UNDER CONSTRUCTION

During this quarter (Fall 2022), I am currently enrolled in the engineering course E96R. This course is meant to teach students about the rocket desinging process, and to familiarize students with common engineering tools and applications. In this course, I have contributed to the design of two model rockets. This first rocket was completely 3D printed and designed on SolidWorks. After experimenting with an application called OpenRocket, my partner and I settled on a stream-line desing with trapezoidal fins and a parabolic nose cone. On our second rocket (currently still being designed and manufactured) my partners and I settled on another stream-line design, but this time we did more research into the components of the rocket. We discovered that at the speeds our rocket is projected to achieve, swept and trapezoidal fins are best. Additionally, we found that though many different fineness ratios work, a fineness ratio of about 2 to 2.5 is best for our design. Finally, differently from our first rocket, we are experimenting with a boat-tail. Based on our research, this boat-tail should allow us to achieve a higher apogee and speed. Additionally, we are experimenting with new construction material. Instead of having a completely 3D printed rocket, now our body tube will be made of a fiberglass layup, and out fins, engine mounts, and centering rings will be made out of plywood. Our nose cone and boat tail will likely remain 3D printed. Finally, my team is also going to contruct and code our own altimeter. Using an arduino microcontroller, we will use solder to wire up the rocke for launch and for its altimeter. After integrating all these design components, our rocket is projected to achieve an apogee of around ~2200 ft.

Saddleback Robotics

I joined my local community college's Mars Rover Team to further my understanding of physics and engineering. My subgroup applied physics principles to design the rover's' chassis, drivetrain, and arm. Joining this team allowed me, for the first time, to work collaboratively with other students to solve engineering challenges. Every Sunday during the school year, the whole team would meet, integrate all the subgroups parts, and would focus on designing new parts or assembling the rover. In June 2022, our rover was placed in a competition with rovers designed by students from some of the most prestigious universities in the world.
