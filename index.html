Experience

Design, Build, Fly at UCLA

At UCLA, I have joined the Design/Build/Fly (DBF) club. In Design/Build/Fly (DBF), I decided to join the aerodynmaics subteam. DBF is a club at UCLA that designs and assembles a radio-controlled airplane each year. Each year, the AIAA Design/Build/Fly intercollegiate competiton that we compete in has a different set of missions for our plane to complete. the whole team has frequent general meetings in which each subteam discusses each weeks progress and what they might need done from another subteam. Additionally, we brainstorms about how to best and most efficiently design our airplane. For example, this year our plane is required to have removeable wings that can be reassembled in no more than around 10 mins. Therefore, in our general meetings, we discuss different design ideas as well as how testing of previous designs went. Though this is my first quarter on the team, I feel as though I have already learned so much about plane design and collaboration. As previously mentioned, I was a member of a collaborative engineering project at my community college. However, because this project has many more members and because I have been able to get much more hands-on experience with intricate designs and ideas, DBF has already taught me so much more about the collaboration and the engineering process.

In the aerodynamics subteam, we design the aerodynamic surfaces of the airplane. Though I am still in the "New Member Training" phase, I have already learned how to design a airfoil, how to import it into, and further design it in, SolidWorks from curve points calculated in MS Excel, how to run CFD simulations on airfoils with applications like XFOIL and SimScale, and how to use MATLAB to analyze flight data (from altimeters and speedometers). I look forward to really getting involved with this subteam, and learning more about aerodynamics!

In the CAD subteam, we have are tasked with integrating any design ideas, and getting them ready for either 3D printing or laser cutting (if applicable). Additionally, we also work on designing how certain parts will fit together. For example, we are currently working how to mount the erring to the fuselage. After each subgroup settles on a design for any specific part they have been working on, we create the part in SolidWorks and assemble the plane in an assembly file. This is to ensure that all the plane components fit together as designed, so that subteams can have references for specific components sizes (when designing parts to connect to any alread-designed parts), and to allow the whole team to have a visual of how each component connection should fit together and how they should appear during and after construction.

Engineering Course: E96R Rocket Engineering

Also at UCLA, I decided to enroll in a rocket engineering course. In this course, students were tasked with forming teams to design, test, and assemble two model rockets. Using a model rocket design program called OpenRocket, my team settled on a mainstream design for our first rocket. After 3D modeling it in SolidWorks, with a detachable nose cone and deployable parachute, we then completely 3D printed the rocket. However, when launch came, the results were lack luster and we did not achieve anywhere near the apogee and speed that we expected.

Therefore, when designing our second rocket, we wanted to do better. So, each team member conducted their own independent research and suggested ideas to the group. We incorporated parts of the first rocket that worked well, like the number and shape of the fins, but also added new components and experimented with different materials. We made the body tube out of our own fiberglass lay-up that we created, the fins out of laser cut ply wood, we designed a smaller, more aerodynamic nose cone, we incorporated a new, 3D printed boat tail to increase the speed of our rocket, and also included a new altimeter. It was satisfying to see our hard work payoff when our rocket exceeding our expectations, achieving an apogee of about 3200 ft and a max speed of about 0.5 Mach. I am currently enrolled in a plane engineering course where we will conduct similar work, but with remote-controlled planes.

Saddleback Robotics

At my previous institution, I joined the robotics club's Mars Rover Team. This team was tasked with the ground-up design and assembly of a radio-controller rover that was capable of navigating rough terrain, driving autonomously, picking up and dragging heavy objects with its mechanical arm, and analyzing dirt samples for signs of life. Though I also helped different subteams with assembly, I decided to join the chassis and electrical subteams. On these subteams, I was assigned to design, 3D model, and assemble different components within the rover's chassis. These components mainly consisted of electrical circuits and their corresponding housing boxes, however I also worked on assembling different components that connected to the rover's drivetrain.
